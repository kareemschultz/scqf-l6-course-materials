================================================================================
  HARVARD REFERENCING - QUICK GUIDE FOR SCQF LEVEL 6
================================================================================

  Harvard referencing has two parts:
  1. In-text citations (inside your paragraphs)
  2. Reference list (at the end of your document, alphabetical order)

================================================================================
  IN-TEXT CITATIONS
================================================================================

  Basic format:  (Author Surname, Year)

  Examples:

  One author:
    "Staff motivation can improve productivity (Robertson, 2021)."

  Two authors:
    "The study found mixed results (Clark and Patel, 2022)."

  Three or more authors:
    "Growth was slower than expected (Williams et al., 2023)."

  Direct quote (use sparingly):
    Robertson (2021, p.45) states that "motivation is the single most
    important factor in employee retention."

  Organisation as author:
    "SMEs account for 99% of UK businesses (Federation of Small
    Businesses, 2024)."

  No author:
    "Sales rose by 12% (The Financial Times, 2023)."

  Website:
    "(BBC News, 2024)" or "(gov.uk, 2023)"

  TIPS:
  - Place the citation at the end of the sentence, before the full stop.
  - If you name the author in the sentence, only put the year in brackets:
    "Robertson (2021) argues that..."
  - Never use footnotes for Harvard referencing.

================================================================================
  REFERENCE LIST
================================================================================

  At the end of your document, list every source you cited in alphabetical
  order by author surname. Do NOT include sources you did not cite.

  FORMAT BY SOURCE TYPE:

  Book:
    Surname, Initials. (Year) Title in Italics. Edition. Place: Publisher.

    Example:
    Robertson, A. (2021) Human Resource Management. 4th edn. London:
    Pearson Education.

  Journal article:
    Surname, Initials. (Year) 'Article title', Journal Name in Italics,
    Volume(Issue), pp. page-page.

    Example:
    Clark, D. and Patel, S. (2022) 'Growth strategies for small firms',
    Journal of Business Studies, 15(3), pp. 112-128.

  Website:
    Author or Organisation (Year) Title of page. Available at: URL
    (Accessed: Day Month Year).

    Example:
    Federation of Small Businesses (2024) UK small business statistics.
    Available at: https://www.fsb.org.uk/uk-small-business-statistics
    (Accessed: 10 January 2026).

  Report:
    Organisation (Year) Title of report. Place: Publisher.

    Example:
    Scottish Government (2023) Scotland's Economic Strategy. Edinburgh:
    Scottish Government.

  Newspaper article:
    Surname, Initials. (Year) 'Article title', Newspaper Name in Italics,
    Day Month, p. page.

    Example:
    Thomson, K. (2024) 'High street retailers face new challenges',
    The Scotsman, 15 March, p. 7.

================================================================================
  COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
================================================================================

  1. Not citing sources you used. If you got information from somewhere,
     cite it. Uncited facts look like plagiarism or made-up claims.

  2. Citing sources you did not actually read. Only cite what you used.

  3. Inconsistent formatting. Pick one style and stick with it throughout.

  4. Missing dates. If no date is available, write (no date) or (n.d.).

  5. Using "Anonymous" unnecessarily. Use the organisation name instead.

  6. Forgetting the reference list. In-text citations without a reference
     list are incomplete. The assessor needs to be able to find your sources.

  7. Not alphabetising the reference list. Sort by first author surname A-Z.
