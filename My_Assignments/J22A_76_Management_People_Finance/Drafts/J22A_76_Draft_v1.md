## A1: Three HRM Approaches (15 marks)

Human Resource Management involves the strategic approach to managing an organisation's most valuable asset - its people. Three key HRM approaches are recruitment, training, and development.

### Recruitment

Recruitment is the process of attracting, screening, and selecting qualified candidates for a job within an organisation. Getting recruitment right is crucial because hiring the wrong person is costly - not just in terms of salary, but also lost productivity, training investment, and potential damage to team morale.

**The Recruitment Process:**

1. **Job Analysis:** Understanding what the role involves and what skills are needed
2. **Job Description:** Writing a clear outline of duties, responsibilities, and working conditions
3. **Person Specification:** Defining the essential and desirable qualifications, skills, and experience
4. **Advertising:** Promoting the vacancy through appropriate channels (internal notice boards, job websites, agencies, social media)
5. **Shortlisting:** Reviewing applications and selecting candidates for interview
6. **Selection:** Using interviews, tests, and assessments to choose the best candidate
7. **Offer and Onboarding:** Making the job offer and integrating the new employee

**Internal vs External Recruitment:**

Internal recruitment (promoting existing staff) is cheaper, faster, and motivates employees by showing career progression is possible. However, it limits fresh ideas and may create resentment among those not selected.

External recruitment brings in new perspectives and skills but is more expensive, time-consuming, and carries greater risk as the person is an unknown quantity.

---

### Training

Training focuses on developing the skills employees need to perform their current job effectively. It's an investment that pays off through improved productivity, quality, and employee satisfaction.

**Types of Training:**

**Induction Training:** Given to new employees to help them understand the organisation, its policies, and their role. This might include health and safety briefings, introductions to colleagues, and an overview of company culture.

**On-the-Job Training:** Learning while doing the actual work, often with guidance from an experienced colleague. This is practical and directly relevant but can slow down production and depend heavily on the trainer's abilities.

**Off-the-Job Training:** Taking place away from the normal work environment, such as courses, workshops, or conferences. This allows focused learning without distractions but takes employees away from productive work and can be expensive.

**Benefits of Training:**
- Improved employee performance and productivity
- Better quality of work and fewer errors
- Reduced accidents and improved safety
- Higher employee motivation and job satisfaction
- Reduced staff turnover (people feel valued and invested in)

---

### Development

While training focuses on current job requirements, development takes a longer-term view, preparing employees for future roles and responsibilities. It's about building capability for tomorrow, not just today.

**Development Methods:**

**Mentoring:** Pairing less experienced employees with senior colleagues who provide guidance, advice, and support. The mentor shares their experience and helps the mentee navigate their career.

**Coaching:** One-to-one support focused on specific skills or challenges. Unlike mentoring, coaching often comes from trained professionals rather than senior colleagues.

**Job Rotation:** Moving employees through different roles or departments to broaden their experience and understanding of the business.

**Management Development Programmes:** Structured programmes preparing high-potential employees for leadership roles, often combining formal training with practical projects.

**Why Development Matters:**

Development is essential for succession planning - ensuring the organisation has capable people ready to step into key roles when needed. It also helps retain talented employees who might otherwise leave for better opportunities elsewhere. When people see a clear path for growth within the organisation, they're more likely to stay and commit to its success.

## A2: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (15 marks)

Abraham Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, introduced in 1943, is one of the most influential theories of motivation. Maslow suggested that human needs are arranged in a hierarchy, and people are motivated to fulfil lower-level needs before they can focus on higher-level ones.

### The Five Levels

**Level 1: Physiological Needs**

These are basic survival needs - food, water, shelter, warmth, and rest. In the workplace, this translates to earning enough money to live on.

*Workplace Example:* An employee earning minimum wage is primarily focused on paying rent and buying food. They may accept poor working conditions or boring tasks simply because they need the income. Until this basic financial security is established, higher-level motivators won't have much impact.

*How Employers Address This:* Fair wages, adequate breaks, comfortable working temperature, clean facilities.

---

**Level 2: Safety and Security Needs**

Once basic needs are met, people seek stability and protection from harm. This includes physical safety but also economic security.

*Workplace Example:* An employee worried about redundancy rumours will be anxious and distracted, unable to focus on performing well. Job security, knowing their position is stable, allows them to relax and contribute more effectively.

*How Employers Address This:* Permanent contracts, pension schemes, health insurance, safe working environment, clear policies and procedures.

---

**Level 3: Social/Belonging Needs**

Humans are social creatures who need connection, friendship, and a sense of belonging to groups.

*Workplace Example:* An employee who works remotely and rarely interacts with colleagues may feel isolated and disconnected from the organisation. They might lose motivation even if the pay is good and the job is secure.

*How Employers Address This:* Team-building activities, social events, open-plan offices, collaborative projects, supportive management.

---

**Level 4: Esteem Needs**

People need to feel respected and valued - both self-respect and recognition from others. This includes feelings of achievement, competence, and status.

*Workplace Example:* An employee who consistently delivers excellent work but never receives acknowledgment will eventually become demotivated. A simple "thank you" or public recognition can be more motivating than a small pay rise.

*How Employers Address This:* Recognition programmes, job titles, promotions, responsibility, praise from managers, achievement awards.

---

**Level 5: Self-Actualisation**

The highest level involves reaching one's full potential and achieving personal growth. Not everyone reaches this level, but those who do seek challenging, meaningful work.

*Workplace Example:* A senior manager who has financial security, good relationships, and respect might still feel unfulfilled if their work doesn't challenge them or allow creativity. They may seek new projects, innovations, or even leave to start their own business.

*How Employers Address This:* Challenging assignments, autonomy, opportunities to innovate, career development, purpose-driven work.

---

### Applying Maslow in Management

Managers can use Maslow's theory by recognising that different employees may be at different levels. A graduate in their first job may be focused on level 1 and 2 (paying off student debt, getting a permanent contract), while a senior employee might be more motivated by level 4 and 5 factors (recognition, challenging projects).

The theory reminds us that money isn't the only motivator - and for employees whose lower needs are satisfied, pay rises may be less effective than recognition, interesting work, or team belonging.

**Limitations:** Critics point out that people don't always follow this neat hierarchy. Someone might sacrifice safety for belonging (taking a risky job to work with friends) or pursue self-actualisation before esteem needs are fully met. Cultural differences also affect which needs people prioritise.

## A3: Five Types of Industrial Action (15 marks)

Industrial action refers to steps taken by employees, usually coordinated through trade unions, to put pressure on employers during disputes about pay, conditions, or other workplace issues.

### 1. Strike

A strike is the complete withdrawal of labour - workers refuse to come to work. It's the most dramatic and disruptive form of industrial action.

**How It Works:** Employees stop working entirely, often forming picket lines outside the workplace to discourage others from entering.

**Impact on Organisations:**
- Production stops completely
- Orders cannot be fulfilled; deliveries are delayed
- Revenue is lost
- Customers may switch to competitors
- Reputation damage
- However, workers also lose wages, so strikes are usually a last resort

*Example:* Rail strikes in the UK during 2022-23 caused significant travel disruption and cost the rail industry millions in lost revenue.

---

### 2. Work-to-Rule

Work-to-rule means employees do exactly what their contract requires - no more, no less. They follow every rule and procedure precisely, refusing to use initiative or go beyond strict requirements.

**How It Works:** Instead of using common sense to keep things running smoothly, workers insist on following every regulation to the letter.

**Impact on Organisations:**
- Significant slowdown in work without technically breaking contracts
- Exposes how much organisations depend on employees' goodwill and flexibility
- Difficult for employers to discipline, as workers are following rules
- Frustrating for customers who experience delays

*Example:* Airport security staff following every procedure exactly, leading to longer queues and flight delays.

---

### 3. Go-Slow

A go-slow (or slowdown) involves employees deliberately working at a reduced pace while remaining at work.

**How It Works:** Workers take longer over every task, move more slowly, and reduce their productivity without actually stopping work.

**Impact on Organisations:**
- Reduced output and missed targets
- Hard to prove as deliberate action (workers claim they're being careful)
- Creates frustration for management
- Lower impact than strike but still costly
- Workers continue receiving normal pay

*Example:* Factory workers taking twice as long as usual to complete each assembly, halving daily production.

---

### 4. Overtime Ban

An overtime ban means employees refuse to work any hours beyond their contracted time, even when overtime is usually expected or requested.

**How It Works:** Workers go home exactly when their contracted hours end, regardless of workload.

**Impact on Organisations:**
- Particularly damaging during busy periods (Christmas for retailers, financial year-end for accountants)
- Deadlines may be missed
- May require hiring temporary staff at higher cost
- Workers receive normal pay (just not overtime pay)
- Less disruptive than strikes but can be sustained longer

*Example:* NHS nurses refusing overtime during winter pressures, leaving hospitals short-staffed.

---

### 5. Sit-In

A sit-in (or occupation) involves workers occupying the workplace but refusing to work. They physically remain on the premises, preventing normal operations.

**How It Works:** Employees enter the workplace as normal but instead of working, they occupy the space and refuse to leave.

**Impact on Organisations:**
- Production stops completely
- Prevents employers bringing in replacement workers
- Creates significant media attention
- May raise security and safety concerns
- Can be legally complex (trespass issues)
- Often used as a last resort when redundancies are announced

*Example:* Factory workers occupying a plant scheduled for closure to protest job losses.

---

### Overall Impact of Industrial Action

Industrial action affects organisations in several ways:
- **Financial losses** from reduced production and sales
- **Customer relationships** damaged by delays and uncertainty
- **Employee relations** may become more adversarial
- **Management time** diverted to handling the dispute
- **Reputation** may suffer in the eyes of customers, investors, and potential employees

However, industrial action also serves an important purpose - it gives workers a voice and bargaining power when negotiations have failed. The threat of action often leads to better outcomes through negotiation.

## A4: Impact of Employment Legislation (15 marks)

Organisations must comply with various laws that protect employees' rights. Three significant pieces of legislation are the Health and Safety at Work Act, the Equality Act, and Data Protection legislation.

### Health and Safety at Work Act 1974

This Act establishes employers' duty to ensure the health, safety, and welfare of their employees at work.

**Key Requirements:**
- Provide and maintain safe equipment and systems of work
- Ensure safe handling, storage, and transport of materials
- Provide adequate information, instruction, training, and supervision
- Maintain a safe working environment with safe access and exit
- Provide adequate welfare facilities

**Impact on Organisations:**

*Costs:* Organisations must invest in safety equipment, training, risk assessments, and potentially dedicated health and safety staff. For manufacturing or construction businesses, these costs can be substantial.

*Processes:* Risk assessments must be conducted for all work activities. Records must be kept, and procedures documented. This creates administrative burden but also drives systematic thinking about hazards.

*Benefits:* Despite the costs, good health and safety practices reduce accidents, lowering insurance costs, compensation claims, and lost working time. They also improve employee morale - workers appreciate employers who take their safety seriously.

*Penalties:* Non-compliance can result in prosecution, fines, and even imprisonment for individuals. Reputational damage can also be severe.

---

### Equality Act 2010

This Act protects people from discrimination based on protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation.

**Key Requirements:**
- No direct discrimination (treating someone worse because of a protected characteristic)
- No indirect discrimination (policies that disadvantage certain groups)
- Reasonable adjustments for disabled employees
- Equal pay for equal work
- Prevention of harassment and victimisation

**Impact on Organisations:**

*Recruitment:* Job advertisements, shortlisting, and interview processes must be fair and based on job-related criteria. Asking about age, family plans, or health conditions at interview is risky.

*Policies:* HR policies must be reviewed to ensure they don't inadvertently discriminate. For example, a requirement for all staff to work evenings might disadvantage parents (indirect discrimination).

*Culture:* Organisations must foster inclusive workplaces. Training on equality and unconscious bias is common. Harassment must be taken seriously and dealt with promptly.

*Benefits:* Beyond compliance, diverse and inclusive workplaces tend to perform better. Different perspectives drive innovation, and employees who feel valued are more engaged and loyal.

*Penalties:* Employment tribunals can award compensation with no upper limit for discrimination claims. In 2024, new duties were introduced requiring employers to take "reasonable steps" to prevent sexual harassment, with tribunals able to increase compensation by 25% for breaches.

---

### Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR

This legislation controls how organisations collect, store, and use personal information about employees (and others).

**Key Principles:**
- Data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently
- Collected for specified, explicit purposes
- Adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary
- Accurate and kept up to date
- Not kept longer than necessary
- Processed securely

**Impact on Organisations:**

*Data Handling:* HR departments must be careful about what employee data they collect and how they store it. Personnel files, health records, and performance data all need proper protection.

*Employee Rights:* Employees can request access to their personal data (Subject Access Request), and organisations must respond within one month. Employees can also request correction of inaccurate data.

*Security:* Organisations must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to protect data. This might include encryption, access controls, and staff training.

*Breach Response:* Data breaches must be reported to the Information Commissioner's Office within 72 hours if they pose a risk to individuals.

*Penalties:* Fines can be substantial - up to £17.5 million or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. Beyond fines, data breaches damage trust with employees and customers.

## B1: Three Sources of Finance (10 marks)

Businesses need finance to start up, operate, and grow. Different sources of finance suit different situations.

### 1. Retained Profit

Retained profit is money that the business has earned through its operations and kept within the business rather than distributing to owners or shareholders as dividends.

**How It Works:** If a business makes £100,000 profit and pays £40,000 in dividends, the remaining £60,000 is retained profit, available to reinvest in the business.

**Reasons to Choose Retained Profit:**
- **No cost:** Unlike loans, there's no interest to pay
- **No dilution:** Unlike shares, ownership isn't reduced
- **No approval needed:** The business doesn't need to convince a bank or investor
- **Flexibility:** Can be used for any purpose without restrictions

**Limitations:** Only available if the business is already profitable. Shareholders may be unhappy receiving lower dividends.

---

### 2. Bank Loan

A bank loan is a fixed amount of money borrowed from a bank, repaid over an agreed period with interest.

**How It Works:** The business applies for a loan, the bank assesses the risk, and if approved, provides the funds. The business then makes regular repayments (monthly or quarterly) until the loan is paid off.

**Reasons to Choose a Bank Loan:**
- **Large amounts available:** Can access significant capital for major investments
- **Predictable repayments:** Fixed schedule makes budgeting easier
- **Ownership retained:** Unlike shares, the business owners keep full control
- **Tax benefit:** Interest payments are tax-deductible expenses

**Limitations:** Interest adds to the cost. Repayments must be made regardless of business performance. May require collateral (security). Can be difficult to obtain for new or struggling businesses.

---

### 3. Share Capital

Share capital means raising money by selling shares (portions of ownership) in the company to investors.

**How It Works:** The company issues new shares and sells them to investors. The investors become part-owners and are entitled to a share of future profits (dividends) and usually voting rights.

**Reasons to Choose Share Capital:**
- **No repayment required:** Unlike loans, the money doesn't need to be paid back
- **Large amounts possible:** Can raise substantial sums for major expansion
- **Shared risk:** Investors share the business risk; if it fails, they lose their investment
- **Expertise:** Investors often bring valuable knowledge and connections

**Limitations:** Dilutes ownership - existing owners' share of the business is reduced. Dividends are expected, reducing future profits available for reinvestment. Loss of control if investors gain significant voting power.

## B2: Five Purposes of Financial Statements (15 marks)

Financial statements are formal records of a business's financial activities. They serve multiple important purposes.

### 1. Supporting Investment Decisions

Potential investors need information to decide whether to invest their money in a business. Financial statements provide the data needed to assess:
- Is the business profitable?
- Is it growing?
- How does it compare to competitors?
- What return might I expect on my investment?

Without access to reliable financial statements, investors would be making decisions in the dark, which would make them very reluctant to invest.

### 2. Informing Lending Decisions

When a business applies for a loan, the bank needs to assess whether the business can afford to repay it. Financial statements help lenders understand:
- Does the business generate enough profit to cover loan repayments?
- What assets could serve as collateral?
- How much debt does the business already have?
- Is cash flow healthy enough to make regular payments?

A business with strong financial statements will find it easier to secure loans at better interest rates.

### 3. Enabling Business Planning and Control

Management uses financial statements to run the business effectively:
- Comparing actual performance to budgets and forecasts
- Identifying trends (are sales growing? are costs under control?)
- Making decisions about pricing, investment, and resource allocation
- Setting targets for the coming period

Regular financial reporting helps managers spot problems early and take corrective action.

### 4. Assessing Financial Health

Financial statements provide a snapshot of the business's position:
- What does the business own (assets)?
- What does it owe (liabilities)?
- Is it solvent (can it pay its debts)?
- Is the trend positive or negative?

This information helps identify risks - like cash flow problems or excessive debt - before they become crises.

### 5. Meeting Legal and Tax Requirements

Limited companies are legally required to prepare and file financial statements. These serve several purposes:
- Calculating corporation tax owed to the government
- Providing shareholders with information about their company
- Allowing auditors to verify the accuracy of the accounts
- Ensuring transparency and accountability

Non-compliance can result in fines and legal penalties.

## B3: Five Accounting Ratios and Their Limitations (15 marks)

Accounting ratios transform raw financial data into meaningful measures that can be compared over time or against other businesses.

### 1. Gross Profit Margin

**Formula:** (Gross Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100

**What It Shows:** How efficiently the business is producing or buying what it sells. A higher margin means more money is left after direct costs to cover overheads and generate profit.

**Example:** If a business has revenue of £500,000 and gross profit of £200,000, the gross profit margin is 40%. This means for every £1 of sales, 40p remains after direct costs.

**Interpretation:** Good margins vary by industry. Supermarkets operate on very thin margins (2-5%), while software companies might achieve 80%+.

---

### 2. Net Profit Margin

**Formula:** (Net Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100

**What It Shows:** Overall profitability after ALL costs, including overheads, interest, and tax. This is what's actually left for owners.

**Example:** If revenue is £500,000 and net profit is £50,000, the net profit margin is 10%.

**Interpretation:** Comparing this with gross profit margin reveals how much overhead costs are eating into profits. A high gross margin but low net margin suggests overhead costs need attention.

---

### 3. Current Ratio

**Formula:** Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

**What It Shows:** The business's ability to pay short-term debts. Can it cover what it owes in the next 12 months?

**Example:** If current assets are £150,000 and current liabilities are £100,000, the current ratio is 1.5:1.

**Interpretation:** A ratio of 2:1 is often considered healthy, but this varies by industry. Too low suggests potential cash flow problems; too high might mean assets aren't being used efficiently.

---

### 4. Acid Test (Quick Ratio)

**Formula:** (Current Assets - Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities

**What It Shows:** Like the current ratio but excludes inventory, which might be difficult to convert to cash quickly.

**Example:** If current assets are £150,000, inventory is £60,000, and current liabilities are £100,000, the acid test ratio is 0.9:1.

**Interpretation:** A ratio of 1:1 is often considered the minimum safe level. Below this, the business might struggle to pay debts if it can't sell inventory quickly.

---

### 5. Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)

**Formula:** (Operating Profit ÷ Capital Employed) × 100

**What It Shows:** How effectively the business is using its capital to generate profit. This is often considered the most important ratio for investors.

**Example:** If operating profit is £80,000 and capital employed is £400,000, ROCE is 20%.

**Interpretation:** Should exceed the cost of borrowing. If ROCE is 8% but the business is paying 10% interest on loans, it's destroying value.

---

### Limitations of Accounting Ratios

While ratios are useful, they have significant limitations:

**1. Historical Focus:** Ratios are based on past data. They tell you what happened, not what will happen. A business might have great historical ratios but be facing future challenges not yet visible.

**2. Accounting Policies:** Different businesses may use different accounting methods (for depreciation, stock valuation, etc.), making direct comparison misleading.

**3. Industry Differences:** What's good in one industry may be poor in another. Comparing a supermarket's margins with a software company's makes little sense.

**4. Timing Issues:** Financial statements capture a single moment. A business might have an unusually good or bad position on the reporting date that doesn't reflect its typical situation.

**5. Window Dressing:** Management might manipulate figures before reporting - delaying purchases, accelerating sales, or timing asset sales to make ratios look better.

**6. Non-Financial Factors:** Ratios don't capture customer satisfaction, employee morale, brand strength, or competitive position - all of which affect future success.

**7. Context Required:** A falling ratio isn't necessarily bad (it might reflect deliberate investment), and a rising ratio isn't necessarily good (it might reflect cost-cutting that harms long-term capability).
