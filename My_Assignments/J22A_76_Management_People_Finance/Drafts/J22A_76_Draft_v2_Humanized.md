## A1: Three HRM Approaches (15 marks)

Human Resource Management is basically about getting the people side of business right. Hire the right people, help them do their jobs well, and keep them growing. Three key approaches are recruitment, training, and development.

### Recruitment

Recruitment is finding and hiring people for jobs. Sounds simple, but getting it wrong is expensive - not just the wasted salary, but lost productivity, training costs down the drain, and sometimes damage to team morale when a bad hire creates problems.

**How the process typically works:**

1. **Job Analysis:** Work out what the role actually involves and what skills are needed
2. **Job Description:** Write down the duties, responsibilities, and working conditions
3. **Person Specification:** Define what qualifications, skills, and experience you're looking for - both essential and nice-to-have
4. **Advertising:** Get the word out - job websites, social media, internal notice boards, agencies
5. **Shortlisting:** Go through applications and pick who to interview
6. **Selection:** Interviews, tests, whatever else helps you choose
7. **Offer and Onboarding:** Make the offer and help the new person settle in

**Internal vs External:**

Promoting from within (internal recruitment) is cheaper and faster. Plus, it shows existing staff they can progress, which motivates people. The downside? You're not getting fresh ideas, and the person who didn't get promoted might be bitter.

Hiring from outside brings new perspectives and skills you don't have internally. But it costs more, takes longer, and there's more risk - you don't really know this person like you know your current staff.

---

### Training

Training is about helping employees do their current job better. It costs money upfront, but pays off through better performance, fewer mistakes, and people who feel invested in.

**Different types:**

**Induction Training:** For new starters - showing them around, explaining policies, introducing colleagues, teaching them the systems. Boring but necessary.

**On-the-Job Training:** Learning by doing, usually with someone experienced guiding you. Practical and directly relevant, but can slow things down and depends heavily on how good the trainer is.

**Off-the-Job Training:** Courses, workshops, conferences away from normal work. Allows focused learning without distractions, but takes people away from productive work and can be pricey.

**Why bother with training:**
- People do their jobs better and faster
- Fewer mistakes, better quality
- Fewer accidents (especially important in dangerous jobs)
- Staff feel valued and are more likely to stay

---

### Development

Training is about today. Development is about tomorrow - preparing people for future roles they might move into.

**How it happens:**

**Mentoring:** Pairing someone junior with someone senior who's been around. The mentor shares experience, gives advice, helps navigate career decisions. It's less structured than formal training but often more impactful.

**Coaching:** One-to-one support focusing on specific skills or challenges. Unlike mentoring, coaches are often brought in from outside.

**Job Rotation:** Moving people through different roles or departments to broaden their experience. Helps them understand how the whole business works, not just their little corner.

**Management Programmes:** Structured training preparing high-potential people for leadership. Usually combines classroom learning with real projects.

**Why development matters:**

If you don't develop your best people, they'll leave for somewhere that will. Development also means you've got people ready to step up when senior roles open - succession planning, they call it. Better to promote someone who knows the company than scramble to recruit externally.

## A2: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (15 marks)

Abraham Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs has been around since 1943, and it's still one of the most useful ways to think about what motivates people. The basic idea: we have different levels of needs, and we're more focused on lower levels until those are satisfied.

### The Five Levels

**Level 1: Physiological Needs**

The basics of survival - food, water, shelter, sleep. In work terms, this translates to earning enough to live on.

*In the workplace:* Someone on minimum wage is primarily thinking about paying rent and buying food. They might put up with a terrible job because they need the income. Until basic financial security is sorted, higher-level stuff doesn't really register as motivation.

*What employers can do:* Pay fair wages. Give proper breaks. Keep the workplace at a reasonable temperature. Sounds obvious, but plenty of employers fail on these basics.

---

**Level 2: Safety and Security Needs**

Once basic needs are covered, we start wanting stability and protection from harm - physical safety, but also job security.

*In the workplace:* Someone hearing redundancy rumours will be anxious and distracted. They can't focus on doing great work when they're worried about losing their job. Security allows people to relax and actually perform.

*What employers can do:* Offer permanent contracts where possible. Provide pension schemes, health insurance. Maintain a safe working environment. Have clear policies so people know what to expect.

---

**Level 3: Social/Belonging Needs**

Humans are social. We need connection, friendship, feeling like we belong to something.

*In the workplace:* Someone working remotely who never sees colleagues can feel isolated and disconnected. They might have good pay and job security but still be unhappy and unmotivated because they feel alone.

*What employers can do:* Create opportunities for teamwork. Organise social events. Design workspaces that allow interaction. Build a supportive, friendly culture.

---

**Level 4: Esteem Needs**

We want to feel respected - both self-respect and recognition from others. Achievement, competence, status all matter here.

*In the workplace:* Someone who consistently delivers great work but never hears acknowledgment will eventually burn out or leave. Recognition matters. A simple "thank you" or public praise can motivate more than a modest pay rise.

*What employers can do:* Recognition programmes. Meaningful job titles. Praise from managers. Giving people responsibility and trusting them with important tasks.

---

**Level 5: Self-Actualisation**

The highest level - reaching your full potential, personal growth, doing meaningful work. Not everyone gets here, but those who do are looking for something deeper than just a paycheque.

*In the workplace:* A senior manager who's financially secure, respected, and has good relationships might still feel unfulfilled if their work isn't challenging or meaningful. They might seek new projects, try to innovate, or leave to start something themselves.

*What employers can do:* Provide challenging assignments. Give autonomy. Allow creativity. Connect work to a larger purpose.

---

### Using Maslow in Practice

The useful thing about Maslow is recognising that different people are at different levels. A graduate in their first job is probably focused on levels 1 and 2 - paying off debts, getting a stable contract. A senior employee might be more motivated by level 4 and 5 stuff - recognition, interesting challenges.

The theory reminds us money isn't the only motivator. For people whose basic needs are covered, a pay rise might matter less than feeling valued or having interesting work.

**But it's not perfect:** People don't always follow the hierarchy neatly. Someone might sacrifice safety for belonging (taking a risky job to work with friends). Cultural differences affect what people prioritise too. It's a framework for thinking, not an exact science.

## A3: Five Types of Industrial Action (15 marks)

Industrial action is what happens when employees and employers can't agree on something important - pay, conditions, job security - and workers take steps to put pressure on the employer. Usually this is organised through trade unions.

### 1. Strike

A strike means workers completely stop working. No work, no production. It's the nuclear option.

**How it works:** Employees don't come to work. Often they'll set up picket lines outside to discourage others from entering.

**Impact:**
- Production stops dead
- Orders can't be fulfilled, deliveries miss deadlines
- Revenue disappears
- Customers may go to competitors
- Company reputation takes a hit
- But workers lose their wages too, so strikes are usually a last resort

*Example:* Rail strikes in the UK during 2022-23 caused massive travel disruption and cost the rail industry millions.

---

### 2. Work-to-Rule

Work-to-rule is sneakier than a strike. Workers do exactly what their contract says - nothing more, nothing less. Every single rule gets followed to the letter.

**How it works:** Instead of using common sense to keep things moving, workers insist on following every procedure precisely. No flexibility, no initiative, no "just this once."

**Impact:**
- Major slowdowns without technically breaking any rules
- Hard for employers to discipline - "We're just following the rules!"
- Frustrates customers who experience delays
- Exposes how much organisations rely on workers going above and beyond

*Example:* Airport security following every procedure exactly, creating huge queues and flight delays.

---

### 3. Go-Slow

A go-slow is exactly what it sounds like - workers deliberately work at a reduced pace.

**How it works:** Everything takes longer. More careful over every task. Moving slower. Not technically refusing to work, just... not hurrying.

**Impact:**
- Output drops, targets get missed
- Hard to prove it's deliberate (workers can claim they're being thorough)
- Creates frustration for management
- Workers still get paid normal wages

*Example:* Factory workers taking twice as long over each assembly, halving daily production.

---

### 4. Lockout

A lockout is the opposite of a strike - it's action taken by the EMPLOYER, not the employees. Management prevents workers from entering the workplace.

**How it works:** The employer shuts the gates. Workers who want to work can't get in. It's the employer's version of a strike, used to pressure workers during disputes.

**Impact:**
- Workers lose wages while locked out
- Can break the momentum of union negotiations
- Puts financial pressure on employees to accept employer's terms
- Production stops (but that's the employer's choice, not the workers')
- Can generate negative publicity for the employer
- May strengthen worker solidarity if seen as unfair

*Example:* In professional sports, team owners sometimes lock out players during contract negotiations - the 2011 NBA lockout lasted 161 days while owners and players argued over revenue sharing.

**Why employers use it:**
- Forces workers to feel the financial pain of no wages
- Takes control of timing (employer chooses when, rather than waiting for workers to strike)
- May be used pre-emptively if strike seems likely

---

### 5. Picketing

Picketing means workers stand outside the workplace (usually at entrances) to protest and discourage others from entering.

**How it works:** Striking or protesting workers gather at workplace gates or entrances. They hold signs, hand out leaflets, and try to persuade others (workers, delivery drivers, customers) not to cross the "picket line."

**Impact:**
- Creates visible public protest
- Discourages deliveries - lorry drivers may refuse to cross picket lines out of solidarity
- Generates media attention and public awareness
- Non-striking workers may feel pressured not to enter
- Can damage company reputation if images of angry workers spread
- May deter customers from using services

*Example:* During the 2022-23 UK rail strikes, RMT union members picketed outside stations with placards explaining their dispute. The media images of workers outside stations became a key part of public awareness of the dispute.

**Legal limits:**
- In the UK, picketing must be peaceful
- Limited to six people at each entrance
- Cannot block access or intimidate
- Must be at or near the worker's own place of work

---

### Overall Effects on Organisations

Industrial action hurts organisations through:
- **Lost money** from reduced production and sales
- **Damaged relationships** with customers who can't get what they need
- **Poisoned workplace relations** - resentment can linger long after action ends
- **Management distraction** - dealing with disputes takes time from running the business
- **Reputation damage** - publicly fighting with workers doesn't look good

But industrial action also serves a purpose. It gives workers power when negotiations have failed. The threat of action often leads to better outcomes before things escalate. It's a check on employers taking advantage.

## A4: Impact of Employment Legislation (15 marks)

Organisations must follow numerous laws that protect employees. The assessment requires coverage of five key areas: Equality & Diversity, Health & Safety, Minimum Wage, Working Time Regulations, and Dismissal & Redundancy laws.

### Health and Safety at Work Act 1974

This law says employers must take reasonable steps to protect the health and safety of their workers.

**What employers must do:**
- Provide and maintain safe equipment
- Ensure materials are handled and stored safely
- Give proper training and supervision
- Maintain safe working environments
- Provide adequate facilities

**Impact on organisations:**

*The costs:* Safety equipment, training, risk assessments, possibly dedicated health and safety staff. For industries like construction or manufacturing, this can be substantial.

*The processes:* Risk assessments for every work activity. Documentation. Record-keeping. It's bureaucratic but forces systematic thinking about hazards.

*The benefits:* Fewer accidents means lower insurance costs, fewer compensation claims, less lost working time. Workers who know their employer takes safety seriously are more committed.

*The penalties:* Non-compliance can mean prosecution, big fines, even prison for individuals in serious cases. Reputation damage can be severe too.

---

### Equality Act 2010

This protects people from discrimination based on protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage/civil partnership, pregnancy, race, religion, sex, and sexual orientation.

**What employers must do:**
- No direct discrimination (treating someone worse because of who they are)
- No indirect discrimination (policies that unfairly disadvantage certain groups)
- Make reasonable adjustments for disabled employees
- Pay equally for equal work
- Prevent harassment and victimisation

**Impact on organisations:**

*Recruitment:* Job ads, shortlisting, interviews all need to be fair and based on job-related criteria. Asking about age, family plans, or health at interview is dangerous territory.

*Policies:* Every HR policy needs reviewing. A rule that everyone must work evenings might disadvantage parents (indirect discrimination).

*Culture:* Organisations need inclusive workplaces. Training on equality and unconscious bias is common. Harassment complaints need taking seriously.

*Benefits:* Beyond just avoiding lawsuits, diverse workplaces often perform better. Different perspectives spark better ideas. People who feel included are more engaged.

*Penalties:* Tribunal awards for discrimination have no upper limit. New rules in 2024 require employers to actively prevent sexual harassment, with tribunals able to increase compensation by 25% for failures.

---

### Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR

This controls how organisations handle personal information - including employee data.

**Key principles:**
- Process data lawfully, fairly, and transparently
- Collect only for specific purposes
- Keep it adequate, relevant, not excessive
- Maintain accuracy
- Don't keep it longer than needed
- Process securely

**Impact on organisations:**

*Handling employee data:* HR departments must be careful about what they collect and how they store it. Personnel files, health records, performance reviews all need proper protection.

*Employee rights:* Staff can request access to their personal data (Subject Access Request) and must get a response within a month. They can also ask for corrections.

*Security:* Organisations need appropriate technical measures - encryption, access controls, staff training on data handling.

*Breach reporting:* If data is compromised, the Information Commissioner's Office must be notified within 72 hours if there's risk to individuals.

*Penalties:* Fines up to £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover - whichever is higher. And beyond fines, data breaches destroy trust.

---

### National Minimum Wage and National Living Wage

The National Minimum Wage Act 1998 (and subsequent updates) sets the minimum hourly rate employers must pay workers. The National Living Wage is the higher rate for workers aged 21 and over.

**Current rates (April 2025):**
- 21 and over (National Living Wage): £11.44 per hour
- 18-20: £8.60 per hour
- Under 18: £6.40 per hour
- Apprentice rate: £6.40 per hour

**Impact on organisations:**

*Wage costs:* For businesses employing many minimum wage workers (retail, hospitality, care homes), increases to the minimum wage directly hit the bottom line. Some businesses have struggled to absorb annual increases.

*Compliance burden:* Must track hours carefully to ensure average pay meets minimum. Complex for businesses with tips, accommodation, or variable hours.

*Recruitment:* Can't compete on low wages - must offer at least the legal minimum. Levels the playing field somewhat.

*Penalties:* Employers who underpay face naming and shaming, back-pay orders, and fines of up to 200% of arrears owed. Reputational damage can be severe.

*Business model effects:* Some businesses have automated or reduced services rather than pay higher wages. Self-checkout in supermarkets, for instance.

---

### Working Time Regulations 1998

These regulations (implementing EU Working Time Directive) limit working hours and guarantee rest periods.

**Key provisions:**
- Maximum 48-hour average working week (though workers can opt out)
- Minimum 11 consecutive hours rest per day
- Minimum 24 hours uninterrupted rest per week (or 48 hours per fortnight)
- 20-minute break if working more than 6 hours
- Minimum 5.6 weeks paid annual leave (28 days for full-time workers)
- Special protections for night workers

**Impact on organisations:**

*Scheduling complexity:* Must carefully plan rotas to ensure rest breaks and maximum hours aren't breached. Especially challenging in healthcare, hospitality, and emergency services where 24/7 coverage is needed.

*Record keeping:* Need to track hours worked, especially for workers in multiple roles or locations. The opt-out for the 48-hour week requires written agreement and records.

*Holiday management:* Must ensure all workers take their entitled leave. Can't just pay in lieu (except when employment ends). Need systems to track accrual and usage.

*Cost implications:* May need more staff to cover shifts while ensuring everyone gets proper breaks. Overtime limitations mean can't just work existing staff harder.

*Health and safety:* These regulations exist because tired workers make mistakes and have accidents. Compliance actually benefits organisations through fewer errors and accidents.

---

### Dismissal and Redundancy Laws

Employment Rights Act 1996 and related legislation protect employees from unfair dismissal and set rules for redundancy.

**Fair vs Unfair Dismissal:**

To be fair, dismissal must be for a valid reason:
- Capability (including health)
- Conduct (misconduct or gross misconduct)
- Redundancy
- Statutory illegality (e.g., lost driving licence for a driver role)
- Some other substantial reason

AND the employer must follow a fair procedure (investigation, hearing, right to appeal).

**Redundancy requirements:**
- Genuine business reason (role no longer needed, not just wanting rid of someone)
- Fair selection criteria (objective, not discriminatory)
- Consultation with affected employees (and unions if 20+ redundancies)
- Statutory redundancy pay for employees with 2+ years' service
- Consideration of suitable alternative employment

**Impact on organisations:**

*Can't just fire people:* Must have valid reasons and follow proper procedures. Makes impulsive dismissals risky and expensive.

*Documentation essential:* Need records of warnings, performance issues, investigations. Without evidence of fair process, tribunals will find unfair dismissal.

*Restructuring costs:* Redundancy isn't free - statutory payments plus often enhanced packages to avoid legal challenges and maintain morale.

*Procedural burden:* Consultation periods, selection processes, and appeals take time. Quick workforce changes are difficult.

*Tribunal risk:* Unfair dismissal claims can result in compensation up to a year's salary (or more in discrimination cases). Even winning cases costs legal fees.

*Positive effects:* These protections mean employees feel more secure, which can improve commitment and reduce turnover. The process also forces managers to address performance issues properly rather than ignoring them.

## B1: Three Sources of Finance (15 marks)

Businesses need money to start, operate, and grow. Where that money comes from matters a lot.

### 1. Retained Profit

Retained profit is money the business made and kept instead of paying it out to owners.

**How it works:** Business makes £100,000 profit. Pays £40,000 in dividends. The £60,000 left over is retained profit - available to reinvest.

**Why choose it:**
- **Free money:** No interest payments like with loans
- **Keep ownership:** No giving away shares to investors
- **No permission needed:** Don't have to convince anyone to lend or invest
- **Use it however you want:** No restrictions on what it's for

**The catch:** You can only use this if the business is already profitable. And shareholders might grumble about lower dividends.

---

### 2. Bank Loan

Borrow a set amount from a bank, pay it back over time with interest.

**How it works:** Apply for a loan, bank assesses the risk, if approved you get the money. Then you make regular repayments - usually monthly - until it's paid off.

**Why choose it:**
- **Large amounts available:** Can access serious money for major investments
- **Predictable repayments:** Fixed schedule makes budgeting easier
- **Keep ownership:** Unlike selling shares, you don't give away any control
- **Tax advantage:** Interest payments reduce taxable profit

**The catch:** Interest adds to the cost. Repayments are required whether business is doing well or not. May need to offer collateral. New or struggling businesses often can't get loans.

---

### 3. Share Capital

Selling shares - pieces of ownership in the company - to investors.

**How it works:** Company issues new shares and sells them. Investors become part-owners with a share of future profits and usually voting rights.

**Why choose it:**
- **No repayment:** Unlike loans, you don't have to pay the money back
- **Big amounts possible:** Can raise substantial sums for major expansion
- **Shared risk:** If the business fails, investors lose their investment - you don't owe them anything
- **Expertise:** Investors often bring useful knowledge and connections alongside money

**The catch:** You're giving away ownership. Existing owners' share of the business shrinks. Investors expect dividends. If investors get enough shares, they can influence or even control decisions.

## B2: Five Purposes of Financial Statements (15 marks)

Financial statements are formal records of what's happening with a business's money. They serve several important purposes.

### 1. Helping Investment Decisions

People thinking about investing money need information. Is this business profitable? Is it growing? How does it compare to alternatives? What might I earn on my investment?

Without reliable financial statements, investors would be guessing. They'd be much less willing to invest, which would make it harder for businesses to raise capital.

### 2. Informing Lending Decisions

When a business wants a loan, the bank needs to assess whether they'll get their money back. Financial statements answer key questions:
- Does the business generate enough profit to afford repayments?
- What could serve as security if things go wrong?
- How much debt does the business already carry?
- Is cash flow healthy enough for regular payments?

Good financial statements make it easier to get loans at better rates.

### 3. Enabling Business Planning

Managers use financial statements to run the business:
- How does actual performance compare to budget?
- Are we growing or shrinking?
- Which products or services are most profitable?
- Where should we invest resources?

Regular financial reporting helps spot problems early when something can still be done about them.

### 4. Assessing Financial Health

Financial statements show the overall picture:
- What does the business own (assets)?
- What does it owe (liabilities)?
- Can it pay its debts?
- Is the trend positive or negative?

This helps identify risks - cash flow problems, excessive debt - before they become crises.

### 5. Meeting Legal and Tax Requirements

Limited companies have to prepare and file financial statements by law. These are used for:
- Calculating tax owed
- Informing shareholders about their company
- Allowing auditors to verify accuracy
- Ensuring accountability

Get it wrong and there are fines and legal consequences.

## B3: Five Accounting Ratios and Their Limitations (15 marks)

Accounting ratios turn raw financial numbers into meaningful measures you can compare over time or against other businesses.

### 1. Gross Profit Margin

**Formula:** (Gross Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100

**What it tells you:** How efficiently the business produces or sources what it sells. Higher means more money left after direct costs.

**Example:** £500,000 revenue, £200,000 gross profit = 40% margin. For every £1 of sales, 40p is left after paying for the goods sold.

**What's good?** Varies hugely by industry. Supermarkets run on tiny margins (2-5%). Software companies might hit 80%+.

---

### 2. Net Profit Margin

**Formula:** (Net Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100

**What it tells you:** Overall profitability after ALL costs - not just production costs but rent, wages, interest, tax, everything.

**Example:** £500,000 revenue, £50,000 net profit = 10% margin.

**Why it matters:** Comparing this with gross profit margin shows how much overheads are eating into profits. High gross margin but low net margin? Your overheads need attention.

---

### 3. Current Ratio

**Formula:** Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

**What it tells you:** Can the business pay what it owes in the next 12 months?

**Example:** £150,000 current assets, £100,000 current liabilities = 1.5:1 ratio.

**What's good?** Around 2:1 is often considered healthy, but varies by industry. Too low suggests cash flow problems. Too high might mean assets aren't being used efficiently.

---

### 4. Acid Test (Quick Ratio)

**Formula:** (Current Assets - Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities

**What it tells you:** Same as current ratio, but strips out inventory. Why? Because you might not be able to sell stock quickly if you need cash urgently.

**Example:** £150,000 current assets, £60,000 inventory, £100,000 liabilities = 0.9:1.

**What's good?** 1:1 is often the minimum comfortable level. Below that, if customers don't pay and you can't shift stock fast, you're in trouble.

---

### 5. Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)

**Formula:** (Operating Profit ÷ Capital Employed) × 100

**What it tells you:** How effectively the business uses its capital to generate profit. Often considered the most important ratio for investors.

**Example:** £80,000 operating profit, £400,000 capital employed = 20% ROCE.

**What's good?** Should definitely exceed the cost of borrowing. If you're paying 10% interest on loans but only making 8% on capital, you're actually destroying value.

---

### Why Ratios Aren't the Whole Story

Ratios are useful, but they have serious limitations:

**1. Looking backwards:** Ratios are based on historical data. They tell you what happened, not what will happen. A company might have great ratios but be heading for trouble that's not yet visible.

**2. Apples and oranges:** Different companies use different accounting methods (how they calculate depreciation, value stock, etc.). Comparing them directly can be misleading.

**3. Industry differences:** What's good in one industry is terrible in another. A 5% net margin might be excellent for a supermarket but poor for a software company.

**4. Snapshot in time:** Financial statements capture one specific moment. The business might have had an unusually good or bad day.

**5. Window dressing:** Companies can manipulate figures before reporting - delaying purchases, accelerating sales, timing things to make ratios look better. It's not necessarily illegal, but it can mislead.

**6. Missing the non-financial:** Ratios don't capture customer satisfaction, employee morale, brand strength, or competitive position. These all affect future success.

**7. Context is everything:** A falling ratio isn't necessarily bad (might reflect deliberate investment). A rising ratio isn't necessarily good (might reflect harmful cost-cutting). You need the story, not just the numbers.
