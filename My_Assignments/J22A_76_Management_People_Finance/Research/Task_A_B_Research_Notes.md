## TASK A - PEOPLE MANAGEMENT

### A1: Three HRM Approaches

**1. RECRUITMENT**
- Process of attracting and selecting candidates for employment
- Methods: Internal (promotion, job posting) vs External (advertising, agencies, online)
- Steps: Job analysis → Job description → Person specification → Advertising → Shortlisting → Interviews → Selection

**2. TRAINING**
- Developing employee skills for current role
- Types:
  - Induction training (new employees)
  - On-the-job (learning while working)
  - Off-the-job (courses, workshops, external)
- Benefits: Improved productivity, quality, motivation, reduced accidents

**3. DEVELOPMENT**
- Preparing employees for future roles
- Focus on long-term career growth
- Methods: Mentoring, coaching, job rotation, management training
- Benefits: Succession planning, employee retention, adaptability

### A2: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (5 LEVELS)

**Theory:** People are motivated by needs in a hierarchy. Lower needs must be met before higher needs become motivators.

| Level | Need | Workplace Example |
|-------|------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Physiological** | Fair wages to afford food/shelter, breaks, comfortable workspace |
| 2 | **Safety/Security** | Job security, safe working conditions, pension, health insurance |
| 3 | **Social/Belonging** | Teamwork, friendly colleagues, social events, good working relationships |
| 4 | **Esteem** | Recognition, job titles, praise, responsibility, promotions |
| 5 | **Self-Actualisation** | Challenging work, creativity, autonomy, personal growth, reaching potential |

**Key Points:**
- Lower needs must be satisfied first
- Once a need is met, it no longer motivates
- People can be at different levels
- Managers should identify which level employees are at

**Limitations:**
- Not everyone follows the same hierarchy
- Cultural differences affect priorities
- People may pursue multiple needs simultaneously

### A3: Five Types of Industrial Action

**1. STRIKE**
- Complete withdrawal of labour
- Most disruptive form of action
- Workers lose wages; employers lose production
- Example: Rail strikes in UK (2022-23)

**2. WORK-TO-RULE**
- Employees do minimum required by contract
- Follow every rule strictly, no flexibility
- Slows down work without breaking contract
- Example: Air traffic controllers following every safety check to the letter

**3. GO-SLOW (Slowdown)**
- Deliberately working at reduced pace
- Reduces productivity but workers still present
- Hard to prove as misconduct
- Example: Factory workers taking longer over each task

**4. OVERTIME BAN**
- Refusing to work beyond contracted hours
- Particularly effective in busy periods
- Workers still receive normal pay
- Example: Nurses refusing overtime during winter NHS pressures

**5. SIT-IN**
- Workers occupy workplace but don't work
- Prevents replacement workers being brought in
- Production stops completely
- Example: Factory workers occupying plant during closure disputes

**Impact on Organisations:**
- Lost production and revenue
- Damaged customer relationships
- Increased costs (missed deadlines, overtime later)
- Damaged reputation
- Strained employee relations
- May lead to negotiation and resolution

### A4: Employment Legislation

**1. HEALTH AND SAFETY AT WORK ACT 1974**

**Requirements:**
- Employers must ensure safe workplace
- Provide safe equipment and systems
- Give training on health and safety
- Maintain safe access/exit
- Employees must follow safety procedures

**Business Impact:**
- Cost of compliance (equipment, training)
- Risk assessments required
- Records must be kept
- Fines for non-compliance
- Improved productivity from fewer accidents

**2. EQUALITY ACT 2010**

**Protected Characteristics:**
Age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage/civil partnership, pregnancy, race, religion, sex, sexual orientation

**Requirements:**
- No discrimination in recruitment, pay, promotion, dismissal
- Reasonable adjustments for disabled employees
- Equal pay for equal work
- Prevent harassment and victimisation

**Business Impact:**
- Must review all HR policies
- Training on equality required
- Fair recruitment processes needed
- Risk of tribunal claims if breached
- Promotes diverse, inclusive workplace

**3. DATA PROTECTION ACT 2018 / UK GDPR**

**Key Principles:**
- Data must be processed lawfully, fairly, transparently
- Collected for specific purposes only
- Adequate, relevant, not excessive
- Accurate and kept up to date
- Not kept longer than necessary
- Processed securely

**Business Impact:**
- Must have legal basis for processing employee data
- Employees can request their data (Subject Access Request)
- Heavy fines for breaches (up to £17.5m or 4% of turnover)
- Must report breaches to ICO within 72 hours
- Requires data protection officer for large organisations

## TASK B - FINANCE MANAGEMENT

### B1: Three Sources of Finance

**1. RETAINED PROFIT (Internal)**
- Profit kept in business rather than paid as dividends
- "Free" - no interest or repayment
- Keeps full ownership
- But: Only available if business is profitable; may upset shareholders wanting dividends

**Why Choose:** Best for established businesses wanting to fund growth without debt or dilution

**2. BANK LOAN (External)**
- Fixed amount borrowed, repaid with interest over time
- Predictable repayments for budgeting
- Keep full ownership
- But: Interest costs, may need collateral, must repay regardless of profit

**Why Choose:** Good for specific investments with clear returns; when ownership dilution unacceptable

**3. SHARE CAPITAL (External)**
- Selling shares to investors
- No repayment required
- Large amounts possible
- But: Dilutes ownership, shareholders expect dividends, loss of control

**Why Choose:** Best for major expansion when debt not feasible; bringing in expertise alongside investment

### B2: Five Purposes of Financial Statements

**1. INVESTMENT DECISIONS**
- Investors use statements to evaluate whether to invest
- Compare profitability, growth, returns
- Assess risk vs reward

**2. LENDING DECISIONS**
- Banks use statements to assess creditworthiness
- Check ability to repay loans
- Evaluate collateral and debt levels

**3. BUSINESS PLANNING**
- Management uses statements for strategic planning
- Set budgets and forecasts
- Monitor performance against targets

**4. ASSESSING FINANCIAL HEALTH**
- Understand current position (assets, liabilities)
- Identify trends (improving/declining performance)
- Spot potential risks (cash flow issues, high debt)

**5. LEGAL/TAX COMPLIANCE**
- Required by law for limited companies
- Used to calculate tax liabilities
- Auditors use for verification
- Shareholders entitled to receive them

### B3: Five Accounting Ratios + Limitations

**1. GROSS PROFIT MARGIN**
- Formula: (Gross Profit / Revenue) x 100
- Shows: Efficiency of production/purchasing
- Good value: Varies by industry (retail ~25%, services ~50%+)

**2. NET PROFIT MARGIN**
- Formula: (Net Profit / Revenue) x 100
- Shows: Overall profitability after all expenses
- Good value: Again varies, but higher = better

**3. CURRENT RATIO**
- Formula: Current Assets / Current Liabilities
- Shows: Ability to pay short-term debts
- Good value: Around 2:1 (but varies by industry)

**4. ACID TEST (Quick Ratio)**
- Formula: (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities
- Shows: Ability to pay debts without selling stock
- Good value: Around 1:1

**5. RETURN ON CAPITAL EMPLOYED (ROCE)**
- Formula: (Operating Profit / Capital Employed) x 100
- Shows: How efficiently capital is used
- Good value: Should exceed cost of borrowing

**LIMITATIONS OF RATIOS:**

1. **Historical Data** - Based on past performance, cannot predict future
2. **Different Accounting Policies** - Companies may use different methods, making comparison difficult
3. **Industry Differences** - "Good" values vary significantly by sector
4. **Inflation Effects** - Can distort comparisons over time
5. **Timing** - Snapshot in time may not represent typical position
6. **Non-Financial Factors** - Don't capture customer satisfaction, employee morale, brand value
7. **Window Dressing** - Companies may manipulate figures before reporting date
