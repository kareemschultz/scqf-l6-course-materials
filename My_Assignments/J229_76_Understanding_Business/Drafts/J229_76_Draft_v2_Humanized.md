## A1: Comparing Features of Three Large Organisations from Different Sectors (10 marks)

Large organisations don't all work the same way. Depending on which sector they're in - private, public, or third sector - they have completely different purposes, ownership structures, and ways of operating. Looking at three very different organisations helps show these differences clearly.

### Tata Motors (Private Sector)

Tata Motors is one of India's biggest car manufacturers, established back in 1945 and based in Mumbai. As a private sector company, its main job is making money for the people who own it - its shareholders.

**What defines Tata Motors:**
- **Who owns it:** Shareholders, through the holding company Tata Sons. You can buy and sell Tata shares on Indian stock exchanges
- **Where the money comes from:** Selling cars, issuing shares, bank loans, and reinvesting profits
- **Who calls the shots:** A board of directors representing shareholders
- **What drives decisions:** Ultimately, it's about returns for investors
- **How big:** Over 58,000 employees globally, and they own Jaguar Land Rover too

### NHS Scotland (Public Sector)

NHS Scotland is the publicly-funded healthcare system for Scotland, set up by the National Health Service (Scotland) Act back in 1947.

**What defines NHS Scotland:**
- **Who owns it:** The government, on behalf of all Scottish citizens
- **Where the money comes from:** Entirely from taxes - when you go to the doctor or hospital, you don't pay at the time
- **Who calls the shots:** NHS Boards that answer to Scottish Ministers
- **What drives decisions:** Providing healthcare, not making profit
- **How big:** Around 160,000 staff, £17 billion annual budget, 14 regional boards

### Oxfam (Third Sector)

Oxfam is an international charity that started in Oxford in 1942, now working in over 90 countries around the world.

**What defines Oxfam:**
- **Who owns it:** Nobody, really - it's run by trustees on behalf of the people it helps
- **Where the money comes from:** Donations, charity shops, grants, fundraising
- **Who calls the shots:** Trustees following the charitable objectives
- **What drives decisions:** Ending poverty - not profit, not politics
- **How big:** 20+ member organisations, reached 14.3 million people last year

### Putting Them Side by Side

| Feature | Tata Motors | NHS Scotland | Oxfam |
|---------|-------------|--------------|-------|
| Sector | Private | Public | Third |
| Purpose | Make profit | Deliver services | Social good |
| Ownership | Shareholders | Government | Trustees |
| Funding | Sales, shares | Taxation | Donations |
| Accountable to | Shareholders | Ministers/citizens | Beneficiaries |

What strikes me is how the same activity - running a large organisation - looks completely different depending on why it exists. Tata's decisions ultimately come back to "will this make money?", NHS Scotland asks "will this help patients?", and Oxfam considers "will this reduce poverty?"

## Task 1.1.2: Comparing Structural Characteristics of Business Types (6 marks)

Beyond sectors, businesses can be structured in different legal forms. Here's a comparison of three common types:

### Private Limited Company (Ltd)

A private limited company is owned by shareholders but shares cannot be sold to the general public. Many family businesses and medium-sized companies use this structure.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Ownership:** Shares held privately by founders, family, or selected investors
- **Control:** Directors appointed by shareholders; often the owners run the company themselves
- **Liability:** Limited - shareholders only risk what they invested, not personal assets
- **Share trading:** Cannot sell shares on stock exchange; must find private buyers
- **Capital raising:** Limited to private investment, bank loans, retained profits
- **Transparency:** Less reporting requirements than PLCs; accounts filed but less scrutiny

**Example:** Dyson - the vacuum cleaner company - remained a private limited company for years, which let founder James Dyson make long-term R&D investments without pressure from public shareholders demanding quick returns.

### Public Limited Company (PLC)

A PLC can sell shares to anyone through a stock exchange. This gives access to massive amounts of capital but comes with strings attached.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Ownership:** Anyone can buy shares; ownership can be widely dispersed
- **Control:** Professional managers appointed by board; shareholders vote on major decisions
- **Liability:** Limited for shareholders
- **Share trading:** Freely traded on stock exchanges; share price fluctuates daily
- **Capital raising:** Can raise huge sums through share issues and bonds
- **Transparency:** Heavily regulated; must publish detailed accounts, annual reports, executive pay

**Example:** Tata Motors is listed on the BSE and NSE. This gives them access to capital markets but means quarterly results are scrutinised by analysts and any stumble tanks the share price.

### Franchise

A franchise is a licensing arrangement where a franchisee pays to use an established brand's name, systems, and support.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Ownership:** Franchisee owns their specific outlet; franchisor owns the brand and system
- **Control:** Franchisee controls daily operations but must follow franchisor's rules exactly
- **Liability:** Franchisee takes the financial risk for their outlet
- **Investment:** Lower than starting from scratch - you're buying a proven model
- **Support:** Training, marketing, supply chains provided by franchisor
- **Limitations:** Less freedom; can't just change the menu or redecorate as you like

**Example:** Most McDonald's restaurants are franchises. The franchisee invests around £400,000-£1.5m to open one, follows McDonald's exact procedures, and pays ongoing royalties - but they get a proven brand and system rather than building from nothing.

### Comparison Table

| Aspect | Private Ltd | PLC | Franchise |
|--------|-------------|-----|-----------|
| Capital access | Limited | Extensive | Moderate (personal investment) |
| Control | High (owners) | Distributed | Constrained by franchisor |
| Risk | Limited liability | Limited liability | Personal financial risk |
| Flexibility | High | Moderate (shareholder pressure) | Low (must follow system) |
| Entry barrier | Moderate | High (minimum capital, regulations) | Varies by brand |

## Task 1.1.3: Organic vs Inorganic Growth (6 marks)

Businesses can grow in two fundamental ways: organically (internally) or inorganically (externally through mergers, acquisitions, and partnerships).

### Organic (Internal) Growth

Growing by expanding what you already do - more production, more customers, more locations - using your own resources.

**Example 1: A Local Bakery Increasing Production Capacity**

A successful neighbourhood bakery decides to grow by buying bigger ovens, hiring more bakers, and extending opening hours. This is organic growth in its simplest form.

- **Why choose this method?** The bakery owner maintains complete control, grows at a manageable pace, and can fund expansion from profits rather than taking on debt or partners
- **Access to technology:** Limited - depends on what they can afford
- **Speed of expansion:** Slow and steady
- **Investment required:** Relatively low, can be funded from cash flow
- **Risk:** Low - gradual, reversible steps
- **New market entry:** Limited to local area

**Example 2: Infosys Expanding Through Technology Investment**

Infosys, the Indian IT giant, grew organically for years by continuously investing in new capabilities, training employees, and building delivery centres. Rather than buying other companies, they developed expertise in-house.

- **Why choose this method?** Maintained the distinctive Infosys culture; controlled quality; avoided integration headaches
- **Access to technology:** Built internally through R&D investment
- **Speed of expansion:** Slower but more controlled
- **Investment required:** Significant but spread over time
- **Risk:** Lower - no "bet the company" acquisitions
- **New market entry:** Gradual, establishing offices in new countries over time

### Inorganic (External) Growth

Growing by acquiring, merging with, or partnering with other businesses.

**Example 3: Tata Motors Acquiring Jaguar Land Rover**

In 2008, Tata Motors bought Jaguar and Land Rover from Ford for $2.3 billion. Overnight, an Indian company owned two iconic British luxury car brands.

- **Why choose this method?** Would have taken decades to build brands with JLR's prestige from scratch
- **Access to technology:** Instantly acquired JLR's engineering expertise and R&D capabilities
- **Speed of expansion:** Immediate - entered luxury car market overnight
- **Investment required:** Massive upfront cost ($2.3 billion)
- **Risk:** High - paid premium price, had to manage integration, took on JLR's existing problems
- **New market entry:** Instant presence in European and North American luxury car markets

The acquisition paid off eventually - JLR now contributes significantly to Tata Motors' profits. But it was risky; Ford sold because JLR was losing money.

**Example 4: Starbucks-Tata Partnership (Joint Venture)**

Rather than entering India alone, Starbucks formed a 50-50 joint venture with Tata in 2012 to create Tata Starbucks.

- **Why choose this method?** Starbucks got local expertise (Tata understood Indian consumers, regulations, supply chains). Tata got a premium global brand without having to build one.
- **Access to technology:** Shared - Starbucks' systems plus Tata's local knowledge
- **Speed of expansion:** Faster than going alone, slower than acquisition
- **Investment required:** Shared between partners
- **Risk:** Moderate - shared risk, but also shared control and shared profits
- **New market entry:** Smoother entry into complex Indian market with a knowledgeable partner

### Comparison

| Factor | Organic Growth | Acquisition | Partnership/JV |
|--------|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| Speed | Slow | Fast | Moderate |
| Control | Full | Full (post-integration) | Shared |
| Risk | Lower | Higher | Moderate |
| Investment | Gradual | Large upfront | Shared |
| Culture | Preserved | Integration challenges | Must align two cultures |
| Technology access | Build yourself | Acquire existing | Share |

## A2: Comparing Organisational Types and Growth Methods (10 marks)

### Organisational Types

**Tata Motors** is a Public Limited Company (PLC). That means anyone can buy shares on the stock exchange. This gives access to huge amounts of capital - if investors believe in the company, they'll put money in. The trade-off is transparency. PLCs have to publish their accounts and face scrutiny from shareholders who want returns.

**NHS Scotland** is what's called a statutory body - it exists because Parliament said it should. The National Health Service (Scotland) Act 1947 literally created it. Its structure isn't designed for commercial efficiency; it's designed by law to deliver healthcare.

**Oxfam** is registered as a charitable foundation. Unlike companies, charities can't take profits out. Any money left over has to go back into the mission. This changes everything about how decisions get made.

### How They've Grown

**Tata Motors - Growing by Buying:**
The biggest example is when Tata bought Jaguar Land Rover from Ford in 2008 for $2.3 billion. Overnight, Tata went from being an Indian car company to owning two of Britain's most famous car brands. They didn't have to build those brands from scratch - they just bought them. More recently, in 2024, they split the company into separate commercial and passenger vehicle divisions, letting each focus on its own market. Smart move - what works for selling trucks is pretty different from selling family cars.

**NHS Scotland - Growing Through Politics:**
NHS Scotland doesn't really "grow" in the business sense. It expands when the Scottish Government decides to put more money in. New hospitals, more staff, expanded services - all of this happens through budget decisions made by politicians based on what the population needs. It's growth driven by policy, not profit.

**Oxfam - Growing Through Partnership:**
Oxfam started as one committee in Oxford during World War II. Now it's a confederation of 20+ independent national organisations working together under the Oxfam International umbrella. This model lets them expand into new countries while respecting local knowledge. The French Oxfam knows France; the Indian Oxfam knows India. But they all work together on the global mission.

## A4: Three Internal Structures with Justification (15 marks)

### 1. Flat Organisational Structure

**What it is:**
Few layers of management. The person at the top isn't that far from the person at the bottom. Communication flows more directly.

**Example:** Tech startups often use this - developers might report directly to founders.

**Why organisations use it:**
- **Quick decisions:** Without layers of approval, things happen faster
- **Better communication:** Messages don't get distorted passing through multiple levels
- **Motivated staff:** People feel more involved when they can actually talk to leaders
- **Lower costs:** Fewer managers to pay

**The downsides:**
- Gets messy as the company grows
- Managers end up overwhelmed with too many direct reports
- Less clear career paths (where do you get promoted to?)

**Works best for:** Smaller companies, creative industries, anywhere speed and innovation matter more than strict control.

### 2. Tall/Hierarchical Structure

**What it is:**
Many management levels. Clear chain of command from top to bottom. Each manager supervises a small number of people.

**Example:** The military, big banks, traditional manufacturing companies.

**Why organisations use it:**
- **Crystal clear accountability:** Everyone knows exactly who they report to and who's responsible for what
- **Visible career ladder:** "If I work hard, I can become a supervisor, then a manager, then..."
- **Specialisation:** Each level can focus on their specific area
- **Tight control:** Senior management can maintain close oversight

**The downsides:**
- Slow as molasses for decisions (everything needs multiple approvals)
- Messages get garbled passing through layers
- Departments become silos that don't talk to each other

**Works best for:** Large organisations where control and compliance matter - banking, healthcare, government.

### 3. Matrix Structure

**What it is:**
Combines functional departments (marketing, finance, HR) with project or product teams. People might report to two bosses - their department head and their project manager.

**Example:** Consulting firms, construction companies, multinationals working on complex projects.

**Why organisations use it:**
- **Resource sharing:** Specialists can work on multiple projects without permanent transfers
- **Flexibility:** Teams form and dissolve as needed
- **Knowledge spreads:** People bring expertise from their department to project teams
- **Customer focus:** Can organise around specific clients or products

**The downsides:**
- Two bosses means potential for conflicting priorities
- Power struggles between functional and project managers
- Needs really good communication to work properly

**Works best for:** Complex organisations juggling multiple projects that need diverse expertise.

## Task 1.3.2: Decision-Making Levels and Tools (5 marks)

Large organisations make thousands of decisions. To manage this, they're typically organised into three levels, each handling different types of decisions.

### Three Levels of Decision-Making

**1. Strategic Decisions (Top Management)**

These are the big-picture, long-term decisions that shape the entire organisation's direction. Made by the board of directors, CEO, and senior executives.

**Characteristics:**
- Long time horizon (3-10+ years)
- High risk and uncertainty
- Difficult or impossible to reverse
- Affect the whole organisation
- Require significant resources

**Examples:**
- Should Tata Motors enter the electric vehicle market?
- Should NHS Scotland close regional hospitals and centralise services?
- Should Infosys acquire a company to enter a new market?

**2. Tactical Decisions (Middle Management)**

These implement strategic decisions. Made by department heads, regional managers, and divisional directors.

**Characteristics:**
- Medium time horizon (1-3 years)
- Moderate risk
- Can usually be adjusted if not working
- Affect departments or divisions
- Require significant but bounded resources

**Examples:**
- How should the marketing budget be allocated across products?
- Which suppliers should we partner with for the new product line?
- How many staff should each regional office have?

**3. Operational Decisions (Front-Line Management)**

Day-to-day decisions that keep things running. Made by supervisors, team leaders, and sometimes individual employees.

**Characteristics:**
- Short time horizon (days to months)
- Low risk
- Easily reversible
- Affect specific tasks or small teams
- Require limited resources

**Examples:**
- Which shifts should staff work this week?
- How should today's customer complaints be handled?
- Which supplier invoice should be paid first?

### Decision-Making Tools

Large organisations use various tools to help make better decisions. Two common ones are SWOT analysis and decision trees.

**SWOT Analysis**

SWOT stands for Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. It's a simple framework for assessing a situation before making strategic decisions.

| Internal Factors | External Factors |
|-----------------|------------------|
| **Strengths** - What we do well | **Opportunities** - Favourable external conditions |
| **Weaknesses** - Where we struggle | **Threats** - External dangers |

**Why organisations use SWOT:**
- Forces systematic thinking about internal and external factors
- Simple enough that everyone understands it
- Highlights what needs addressing before moving forward
- Good starting point for strategic planning discussions

**Example:** Before Tata Motors decided to acquire JLR, they would have done a SWOT analysis:
- *Strength:* Strong domestic manufacturing base
- *Weakness:* No premium brand presence
- *Opportunity:* JLR available for purchase
- *Threat:* Global financial crisis affecting car sales

**Decision Trees**

A decision tree is a visual diagram showing choices, possible outcomes, and their consequences. It helps when facing a decision with multiple options and uncertain outcomes.

**Structure:**
- Decision nodes (squares) - where you make a choice
- Chance nodes (circles) - where outcomes are uncertain
- Branches - showing different paths
- End points - showing final outcomes with associated values

**Why organisations use decision trees:**
- Makes complex decisions visual and easier to understand
- Forces you to think through all possibilities
- Allows calculation of expected values for different options
- Documents the reasoning behind decisions

**Example:** A company deciding whether to launch a new product might use a decision tree showing:
- Option 1: Launch now → Success (70% probability, £5m profit) or Failure (30%, -£2m loss)
- Option 2: Delay for more research → Higher success rate but delayed returns
- Option 3: Don't launch → No profit but no risk

By calculating expected values, they can compare options systematically rather than just going with gut feeling.

## B1: Impact of Two Internal Factors (15 marks)

Internal factors are things within the organisation's control that affect how well it performs. Corporate culture and management decisions are two of the biggest.

### Positive Example: Toyota's Corporate Culture

Toyota has been one of the most successful car companies for over 70 years. A big part of that comes down to how they do things - what they call "The Toyota Way."

**Two core ideas drive everything:**
1. **Respect for People** - actually valuing employees and partners
2. **Continuous Improvement (Kaizen)** - never being satisfied, always looking for better ways

**How this creates success:**

**Quality obsession:** At Toyota, any worker on the production line can stop everything if they spot a defect. In most factories, stopping production costs money and workers would get in trouble for it. At Toyota, they'd get in trouble for NOT stopping the line. Problems get fixed immediately rather than passing defective cars to customers.

**Eliminating waste:** The Toyota Production System includes "Just-in-Time" manufacturing - producing only what's needed when it's needed. This sounds obvious but most companies carry mountains of inventory "just in case." Toyota proved you don't have to.

**Everyone's ideas matter:** Because workers feel respected and their ideas are valued, they actually think about improvements. Small continuous improvements add up to huge gains over time. It's not just engineers in a lab coming up with innovations - it's everyone on the floor.

**Result:** Decades of reliability reputation, efficient operations, and workers who genuinely care about the company's success.

---

### Negative Example: Boeing's Management Decisions

The Boeing 737 MAX disaster is a brutal example of what happens when internal decisions go wrong. Two crashes in 2018 and 2019 killed 346 people. This wasn't bad luck - it was the result of choices made inside Boeing.

**What went wrong:**

**Culture flipped from engineering to finance:**
Boeing used to be obsessed with engineering excellence - building the best planes possible. But former CEOs deliberately changed this. One actually said he wanted to run Boeing "like a business rather than a great engineering firm." Profit became the priority. Safety became a cost to be minimised.

**Cutting corners on the 737 MAX:**
To compete with Airbus, Boeing rushed the 737 MAX to market. Rather than design a new aircraft (expensive, slow), they modified an existing design and added software called MCAS to compensate for handling problems. To save airlines money on pilot training, Boeing didn't fully explain this new system to airlines or pilots.

**Ignoring warnings:**
This is the part that really gets me. Boeing's own engineers raised concerns about MCAS. Test pilots experienced problems in simulators. But management dismissed the warnings because addressing them would delay the project and cost money. They even withheld information from regulators.

**The consequences:**
- 346 people dead
- Every 737 MAX grounded worldwide
- $2.5 billion settlement with the US government
- $87 billion wiped off shareholder value
- Reputation possibly destroyed forever

The tragic irony? The cost-cutting that was supposed to protect profits ended up costing far more than doing things properly ever would have.

## B2: PESTEC Analysis - Impact of Two External Factors (20 marks)

External factors are things outside the organisation's control that still affect it. PESTEC looks at Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Competitive factors.

### Netflix - Technological Factors

Netflix wouldn't exist without technology - and technology continues to shape everything they do.

**How technology helps:**

**Streaming itself:**
Netflix started mailing DVDs. The shift to streaming in 2007 changed entertainment forever. But this was only possible because internet speeds improved and compression technology got better. Without reliable broadband reaching homes, streaming would just mean endless buffering. Netflix now reaches 260+ million subscribers worldwide because the technology caught up with the vision.

**AI and personalisation:**
Ever notice how Netflix always seems to know what you might want to watch next? That's machine learning analysing what you've watched, how long you watched it, what you searched for, even when you paused. The recommendation engine reportedly influences 80% of content watched on Netflix. It's creepily accurate, and it keeps people subscribed.

**Their own infrastructure:**
Netflix built a global content delivery network called Open Connect, putting servers close to users around the world. This means less buffering and better quality than competitors who rely on third-party infrastructure. In places with iffy internet, this is a real advantage.

**Technology challenges:**

But technology also creates problems. Netflix faces competition from Disney+, Amazon Prime, Apple TV+, and countless others - all enabled by the same streaming technology Netflix pioneered. TikTok and short-form video platforms are changing how younger people consume content entirely. Netflix can't just keep doing what worked in 2015.

### McDonald's - Social Factors

What people think, believe, and care about massively impacts a consumer business like McDonald's.

**Health consciousness:**

People increasingly know that fast food isn't exactly healthy. Governments run campaigns, schools teach nutrition, documentaries expose the industry. McDonald's has had to respond - adding salads, fruit options, nutritional information on packaging. In some countries they've reduced portion sizes and reformulated recipes to lower salt, sugar, and fat. They can't ignore this trend without losing customers.

**Ethical concerns:**

Modern consumers - especially younger ones - want to know more than just "does it taste good?"
- Where do ingredients come from? (animal welfare, fair trade)
- How are workers treated? (fair wages, safe conditions)
- What happens to the packaging? (plastic waste, recyclability)

McDonald's has invested in sustainable sourcing (certified coffee, palm oil, fish) and committed to recyclable packaging. Whether this is genuine commitment or PR management, I can't say. Probably both.

**Cultural adaptation:**

McDonald's operates in 100+ countries with very different food cultures. In India, where many people don't eat beef for religious reasons, you won't find a Big Mac. Instead there's the McAloo Tikki (potato burger) and Chicken Maharaja Mac. In Japan, teriyaki burgers. This cultural sensitivity is essential for success, but it complicates everything from supply chains to marketing.

**The bottom line:**

Social factors have pushed McDonald's to:
- Innovate menus (healthier options)
- Be more transparent about sourcing
- Invest in sustainability
- Research local markets carefully

Companies that ignore what society cares about eventually pay for it in lost customers.

## B3: Two Stakeholder Conflicts of Interest (15 marks)

Different groups have different interests in any organisation. When those interests clash, someone has to give way or compromise.

### Conflict 1: Shareholders vs Employees

**What's the conflict?**

Shareholders own the company and want returns on their investment - dividends and share price growth. One way to increase profits is to cut costs. And wages are often the biggest cost.

Employees want the opposite - job security, fair pay, good conditions, training, and opportunities to advance. All of these cost money.

**A real example:**

During COVID, lots of companies faced pressure from shareholders to maintain profitability despite the chaos. Some responded by cutting jobs or wages to protect dividend payments.

British Airways cut thousands of jobs while parent company IAG kept paying dividends to shareholders. Workers - who were already dealing with a pandemic and health risks - argued this was deeply unfair. Why should they lose their livelihoods to protect investor returns?

**How it might be resolved:**

- **Balance:** Companies can try to treat both groups fairly rather than maximising one at the expense of the other
- **Long-term thinking:** Demotivated, underpaid workers hurt performance eventually
- **Employee ownership:** Giving workers shares aligns their interests with shareholders
- **Honest communication:** Being upfront about difficult trade-offs rather than pretending they don't exist

### Conflict 2: Business Expansion vs Local Community

**What's the conflict?**

Businesses want to grow - building new facilities benefits shareholders (more profit), employees (more jobs), and often customers (better access to products).

But people living where expansion is planned often have concerns:
- More traffic and congestion
- Noise and pollution
- Changing neighbourhood character
- Pressure on local services
- Threatening existing local businesses

**A real example:**

When Amazon announced plans for its second headquarters (HQ2), cities competed aggressively to win the project with tax breaks and incentives. But when Amazon chose New York's Long Island City, local residents and politicians pushed back hard.

They worried about:
- Housing costs skyrocketing as tech workers moved in
- Already-strained public transport getting worse
- Whether the tax incentives were fair use of public money
- Small local businesses being priced out

The opposition got so intense that Amazon eventually pulled out of New York entirely. A massive corporation with all its resources couldn't overcome local community opposition.

**How it might be resolved:**

- **Actually listen:** Genuine consultation before plans are finalised, not just token meetings
- **Mitigate impacts:** Invest in infrastructure, affordable housing, local hiring
- **Compromise:** Modify plans to address specific concerns
- **Share benefits:** Ensure the local area actually gains from the development, not just the company
