## A1: Comparing Features of Three Large Organisations from Different Sectors (10 marks)

Large organisations operate across three main sectors: private, public, and third sector. Each has distinct characteristics that shape how they function.

### Tata Motors (Private Sector)

Tata Motors is one of India's largest automotive manufacturers, established in 1945 and headquartered in Mumbai. As a private sector company, its primary purpose is to generate profit for its shareholders.

**Key Features:**
- **Ownership:** Owned by shareholders through Tata Sons (the holding company). Shares are traded on the BSE and NSE stock exchanges
- **Funding:** Raised through share sales, bank loans, and retained profits
- **Decision-making:** Board of directors makes strategic decisions on behalf of shareholders
- **Profit motive:** Primary goal is to maximize returns for investors
- **Scale:** Over 58,000 employees globally, owns Jaguar Land Rover

### NHS Scotland (Public Sector)

NHS Scotland is the publicly-funded healthcare system serving Scotland, established by the National Health Service (Scotland) Act 1947.

**Key Features:**
- **Ownership:** Owned by the government on behalf of citizens
- **Funding:** Funded entirely through taxation - services are free at point of delivery
- **Decision-making:** NHS Boards are accountable to Scottish Ministers
- **Service motive:** Primary goal is delivering healthcare, not making profit
- **Scale:** Around 160,000 staff, £17 billion annual budget, 14 regional boards

### Oxfam (Third Sector)

Oxfam is an international charity founded in Oxford in 1942, now operating in over 90 countries worldwide.

**Key Features:**
- **Ownership:** Not owned by anyone - run by trustees on behalf of beneficiaries
- **Funding:** Donations, charity shops, grants, and fundraising events
- **Decision-making:** Trustees make decisions guided by charitable objectives
- **Social motive:** Primary goal is ending poverty, not profit or political agenda
- **Scale:** 20+ member organisations, reached 14.3 million people in 2024-25

### Comparison Summary

| Feature | Tata Motors | NHS Scotland | Oxfam |
|---------|-------------|--------------|-------|
| Sector | Private | Public | Third |
| Purpose | Profit | Service delivery | Social good |
| Ownership | Shareholders | Government | Trustees |
| Funding | Sales, shares | Taxation | Donations |
| Accountability | To shareholders | To ministers/citizens | To beneficiaries |

## A2: Comparing Organisational Types and Growth Methods (10 marks)

### Organisational Types

**Tata Motors** operates as a Public Limited Company (PLC), meaning its shares can be bought and sold by the public on stock exchanges. This gives it access to large amounts of capital but also means it must publish financial reports and face scrutiny from shareholders.

**NHS Scotland** is a statutory body - an organisation created by an Act of Parliament (the NHS Scotland Act 1947). It exists because the law says it must, and its structure is defined by legislation rather than commercial considerations.

**Oxfam** is registered as a charitable foundation. Unlike companies, charities cannot distribute profits to owners. Any surplus must be reinvested in the charitable mission.

### Growth Methods

**Tata Motors - Acquisition and Merger:**
Tata Motors has grown significantly through acquisitions. The most notable was purchasing Jaguar Land Rover from Ford in 2008 for $2.3 billion. This gave Tata instant access to premium car markets worldwide without having to build those brands from scratch. More recently, in 2024, Tata Motors split into separate companies for commercial and passenger vehicles, allowing each division to focus on its specific market.

**NHS Scotland - Government Expansion:**
NHS Scotland grows through government policy and budget allocation. When the Scottish Government decides to expand services - like building new hospitals or hiring more staff - it allocates additional funding from taxation. Growth is driven by political decisions and population needs rather than market forces.

**Oxfam - Federation Model:**
Oxfam has grown by adding new member organisations to its confederation. Starting as the Oxford Committee for Famine Relief in 1942, it now includes 20+ independent national organisations that work together under the Oxfam International umbrella. This allows Oxfam to expand into new countries while respecting local expertise and autonomy.

## A3: Objectives of Four Large Organisations (15 marks)

### 1. Reliance Jio

**Objective: Digital Inclusion**
Reliance Jio's primary objective is to provide affordable internet access to every Indian, regardless of their location or income level. When Jio launched in 2016, internet data in India was expensive and limited. Jio's founder, Mukesh Ambani, believed that cheap internet could transform lives - enabling online education, digital payments, and access to information.

**Why This Objective Matters:**
This objective is important because it aligns with India's national development goals. The government's "Digital India" initiative aims to connect all citizens to the internet. By making data affordable, Jio has helped bring over 465 million subscribers online, many accessing the internet for the first time. This has created new economic opportunities and helped bridge the digital divide between urban and rural India.

### 2. Tata Group

**Objective: Nation Building Through Business**
The Tata Group's overarching objective is to improve the quality of life of communities it serves while building sustainable businesses. Unusually for a private conglomerate, the majority of Tata Sons' shares are held by charitable trusts, meaning profits ultimately support social causes.

**Why This Objective Matters:**
This objective matters because it demonstrates that profit and purpose can coexist. Tata has built schools, hospitals, and research institutions across India. This approach has earned tremendous public trust - the Tata name is synonymous with integrity in India. It shows that companies can contribute to national development while remaining commercially successful.

### 3. Amul

**Objective: Farmer Welfare**
Amul's core objective is to provide fair prices to dairy farmers while delivering affordable milk products to consumers. As a cooperative, Amul returns 85% of every rupee earned back to the farmers who supply the milk - far higher than the global average of 33%.

**Why This Objective Matters:**
This objective is crucial because it protects farmers from exploitation by middlemen. Before Amul, farmers often received very low prices for their milk while traders took most of the profit. The cooperative model ensures that those who produce the milk benefit from its sale. This has lifted millions of rural families out of poverty and made India the world's largest milk producer through the "White Revolution."

### 4. Unilever

**Objective: Sustainable Growth**
Unilever aims to achieve business growth while reducing its environmental footprint. The company has set ambitious targets including net zero emissions by 2039, 100% recyclable packaging by 2025, and implementing regenerative agriculture on 1 million hectares.

**Why This Objective Matters:**
This objective matters because it addresses growing consumer concern about environmental impact. Customers increasingly want to buy from companies that share their values. By investing in sustainability, Unilever protects its long-term business - reducing risks from climate change, resource scarcity, and changing regulations. It also helps maintain the company's reputation among environmentally-conscious consumers.

## A4: Three Internal Structures with Justification (15 marks)

### 1. Flat Organisational Structure

**Description:**
A flat structure has few levels of management between staff and senior leadership. Communication flows more directly, and employees often have more autonomy and responsibility.

**Example:** Many tech startups use flat structures, with developers reporting directly to founders.

**Justification:**
- **Faster decision-making:** With fewer approval layers, decisions can be made quickly
- **Better communication:** Information doesn't get distorted passing through multiple levels
- **Employee empowerment:** Staff feel more involved and motivated when they have direct access to leaders
- **Cost efficiency:** Fewer managers means lower overhead costs
- **Suitable for:** Small to medium organisations, creative industries, startups

**Limitations:**
- Can become chaotic as the organisation grows
- Managers may become overloaded with direct reports
- Less clear career progression paths

### 2. Tall/Hierarchical Structure

**Description:**
A tall structure has many levels of management, with each manager supervising a small number of employees. There is a clear chain of command from top to bottom.

**Example:** The military, large banks, and traditional manufacturing companies typically use tall structures.

**Justification:**
- **Clear accountability:** Everyone knows who they report to and who is responsible for what
- **Defined career paths:** Employees can see clear routes for promotion
- **Specialisation:** Managers can focus on specific areas of expertise
- **Control:** Senior management maintains tight oversight of operations
- **Suitable for:** Large organisations, industries requiring strict compliance (banking, healthcare)

**Limitations:**
- Slow decision-making due to many approval levels
- Communication can be distorted through multiple layers
- Can create "silo" mentality where departments don't collaborate

### 3. Matrix Structure

**Description:**
A matrix structure combines functional departments (like marketing, finance, HR) with project or product teams. Employees may report to two managers - their functional head and their project manager.

**Example:** Large consulting firms, construction companies, and multinational corporations often use matrix structures.

**Justification:**
- **Resource sharing:** Specialists can work across multiple projects without being permanently reassigned
- **Flexibility:** Teams can be assembled and disbanded as projects require
- **Knowledge sharing:** Employees bring expertise from their functional area to project teams
- **Customer focus:** Project teams can be organised around specific clients or products
- **Suitable for:** Complex organisations working on multiple projects simultaneously

**Limitations:**
- Dual reporting can create confusion about priorities
- Power struggles between functional and project managers
- Requires strong communication and coordination

## B1: Impact of Two Internal Factors (15 marks)

Internal factors are elements within an organisation's control that affect its performance. Two significant internal factors are corporate culture and management decisions.

### Positive Example: Toyota's Corporate Culture

Toyota's success over the past 70 years can be largely attributed to its distinctive corporate culture, known as "The Toyota Way."

**The Toyota Way consists of two pillars:**
1. **Respect for People** - valuing employees and partners
2. **Continuous Improvement (Kaizen)** - never-ending pursuit of better methods

**How This Culture Creates Positive Impact:**

**Quality:** Toyota builds quality into every process. Workers are empowered to stop the production line if they spot a defect - something unthinkable in many factories where stopping production costs money. This means problems are fixed immediately rather than passing defective products to customers.

**Efficiency:** The Toyota Production System (TPS) includes "Just-in-Time" manufacturing, producing only what is needed when it is needed. This reduces waste, lowers inventory costs, and improves cash flow.

**Innovation:** The "Kaizen mind" means employees at all levels are encouraged to suggest improvements. Small, continuous improvements add up to significant gains over time. This culture has made Toyota one of the world's most efficient and reliable car manufacturers.

**Employee Engagement:** Because workers feel respected and their ideas are valued, they are more committed to the company's success. This reduces turnover and builds institutional knowledge.

---

### Negative Example: Boeing's Management Decisions

Boeing's 737 MAX crisis demonstrates how poor internal decisions can have devastating consequences. Two crashes in 2018 and 2019 killed 346 people and cost the company billions.

**What Went Wrong:**

**Culture Shift from Engineering to Finance:**
Former Boeing CEOs deliberately changed the company's culture. One CEO stated his intention was to run Boeing "like a business rather than a great engineering firm." This prioritised short-term profits over the engineering excellence that had made Boeing's reputation.

**Cost-Cutting Over Safety:**
To compete with Airbus, Boeing rushed the 737 MAX to market. Rather than design a new aircraft, they modified an existing design and added software (MCAS) to compensate for handling issues. To save money on pilot training, Boeing didn't fully disclose this new system to airlines or pilots.

**Ignoring Warnings:**
Internal engineers raised concerns about the MCAS system. Test pilots experienced problems in simulators. However, management dismissed these warnings because addressing them would delay the project and increase costs. Information about known problems was not shared with regulators.

**Consequences:**
- 346 lives lost
- Global grounding of entire 737 MAX fleet
- $2.5 billion settlement with US government
- $87 billion lost in shareholder value
- Severe damage to company reputation

This case shows how internal management decisions that prioritise short-term profit over safety and quality can destroy value far exceeding any savings made.

## B2: PESTEC Analysis - Impact of Two External Factors (20 marks)

External factors are elements outside an organisation's control that affect its operations. PESTEC analysis examines Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Competitive factors.

### Netflix - Technological Factors

Technology has fundamentally shaped Netflix's business model and continues to drive its success.

**Positive Impacts:**

**Streaming Technology:**
Netflix transitioned from DVD rentals to streaming in 2007, completely transforming how people consume entertainment. Improvements in internet speeds and compression technology have allowed Netflix to deliver high-quality video to 260+ million subscribers worldwide. Without reliable streaming technology, Netflix's current business model would be impossible.

**AI and Personalisation:**
Netflix uses artificial intelligence and machine learning to power its recommendation engine. By analysing viewing habits, search history, and preferences, Netflix can suggest content each user is likely to enjoy. This personalisation keeps subscribers engaged and reduces cancellation rates. The algorithm reportedly influences 80% of the content watched on Netflix.

**Own Infrastructure (Open Connect):**
Netflix built its own content delivery network called Open Connect, placing servers close to users around the world. This reduces buffering, improves quality, and lowers costs. In bandwidth-constrained regions, this gives Netflix an advantage over competitors relying on third-party infrastructure.

**Challenges:**

However, technology also creates challenges. Competition from new streaming platforms (Disney+, Amazon Prime, Apple TV+) has fragmented the market. Short-form video platforms like TikTok are changing how younger audiences consume content, forcing Netflix to experiment with mobile-friendly formats. Technology enables competition as easily as it enables Netflix's own success.

### McDonald's - Social Factors

Social trends significantly impact McDonald's as a consumer-facing food business.

**Health Consciousness:**

Growing awareness of diet-related health issues has created challenges for McDonald's. Consumers, particularly younger generations, increasingly seek healthier food options. This has forced McDonald's to adapt its menu, adding salads, fruit options, and nutritional information. In some markets, they've reduced portion sizes and reformulated recipes to lower salt, sugar, and fat content.

**Ethical and Environmental Concerns:**

Modern consumers care about more than just taste and price. They want to know:
- Where ingredients come from (animal welfare, fair trade)
- How workers are treated (fair wages, working conditions)
- What happens to packaging (recyclability, plastic waste)

McDonald's has responded by committing to sustainable sourcing - certified sustainable coffee, palm oil, and fish. They've set targets for 100% recyclable packaging by 2025. However, as a fast-food company producing enormous volumes of food and packaging, scrutiny remains intense.

**Cultural Adaptation:**

Operating in 100+ countries, McDonald's must adapt to local social norms and tastes. In India, where many people don't eat beef for religious reasons, McDonald's offers the McAloo Tikki (potato burger) and Chicken Maharaja Mac. In Japan, they sell teriyaki burgers. This social sensitivity has helped McDonald's succeed globally, but requires constant adaptation and can complicate supply chains.

**Impact on Strategy:**

These social factors have pushed McDonald's to invest heavily in:
- Menu innovation (healthier options)
- Supply chain transparency
- Sustainability initiatives
- Local market research

Companies that ignore social trends risk losing customers to competitors who better align with changing values.

## B3: Two Stakeholder Conflicts of Interest (15 marks)

Stakeholders are individuals or groups who have an interest in an organisation's activities. Different stakeholders often have conflicting priorities.

### Conflict 1: Shareholders vs Employees

**The Conflict:**

Shareholders invest money in companies and expect returns through dividends and share price growth. To maximise these returns, companies may seek to reduce costs - and wages are often a major expense.

Employees, on the other hand, want job security, fair wages, good working conditions, and opportunities for advancement. These desires often require the company to spend more, not less.

**Real Example:**

During the COVID-19 pandemic, many companies faced pressure from shareholders to maintain profitability. Some responded by cutting jobs or reducing wages to protect dividends. This created direct conflict with employees who were already facing uncertainty and health risks.

In the airline industry, companies like British Airways cut thousands of jobs while parent company IAG maintained its dividend payments to shareholders. Employees and unions argued this was unfair - workers were sacrificed to protect investor returns.

**How This Conflict Might Be Resolved:**

- **Balanced approach:** Companies can seek to balance returns to shareholders with fair treatment of workers
- **Long-term thinking:** Recognising that demotivated, underpaid workers may harm long-term performance
- **Employee ownership:** Some companies give workers shares, aligning their interests with shareholders
- **Transparent communication:** Being honest with both groups about difficult decisions

---

### Conflict 2: Business Expansion vs Local Community

**The Conflict:**

Businesses often want to expand - building new factories, stores, or offices. This growth benefits shareholders (more profit), employees (more jobs), and often customers (better access to products).

However, local communities where expansion is planned may have concerns:
- Increased traffic and congestion
- Environmental impact (noise, pollution)
- Changes to neighbourhood character
- Pressure on local services and infrastructure
- Competition with existing local businesses

**Real Example:**

When Amazon announced plans for its HQ2 (second headquarters), cities competed aggressively to win the project, promising tax breaks and incentives. However, when Amazon selected New York's Long Island City, local residents and politicians protested.

Concerns included:
- Rising housing costs and gentrification
- Strain on public transport
- Whether tax incentives were a fair use of public money
- Impact on small businesses

The conflict became so intense that Amazon eventually cancelled the New York location, showing how powerful community opposition can be.

**How This Conflict Might Be Resolved:**

- **Community consultation:** Genuinely engaging with local residents before finalising plans
- **Impact mitigation:** Investing in infrastructure improvements, affordable housing, or local hiring programmes
- **Compromise:** Modifying plans to address specific concerns
- **Benefits sharing:** Ensuring economic benefits flow to the local area, not just the company
