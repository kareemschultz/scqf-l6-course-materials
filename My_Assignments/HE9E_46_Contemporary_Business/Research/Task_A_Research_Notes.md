## A1: SME Definition (5 marks)

### Official EU Definition (Commission Recommendation 2003/361)

An SME (Small and Medium-sized Enterprise) is defined by the European Commission based on **staff headcount** and **financial thresholds**:

| Category | Employees | Turnover | OR | Balance Sheet |
|----------|-----------|----------|-----|---------------|
| **Medium** | < 250 | ≤ €50 million | OR | ≤ €43 million |
| **Small** | < 50 | ≤ €10 million | OR | ≤ €10 million |
| **Micro** | < 10 | ≤ €2 million | OR | ≤ €2 million |

**Key Rules:**
- The employee threshold MUST be met
- For turnover/balance sheet, the company can meet EITHER threshold (not both required)
- If part of a larger group, may need to include group data

**Economic Significance:**
- SMEs represent 99% of all businesses in the EU
- They generate 2 out of every 3 jobs
- Globally, SMEs make up 90% of all companies and more than 50% of employment

**Sources:**
- [EU SME Definition](https://single-market-economy.ec.europa.eu/smes/sme-fundamentals/sme-definition_en)
- [EUR-Lex SME Definition](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/EN/legal-content/glossary/small-and-medium-sized-enterprises.html)

## A2: 3 Key Features of SMEs (15 marks)

### Feature 1: Flexibility and Agility
- SMEs can adapt to changing market conditions more quickly than large corporations
- Fewer bureaucratic layers allow for quicker decision-making
- Can respond faster to customer needs and market trends
- Example: A local restaurant can change its menu within days based on customer feedback, while a chain like McDonald's takes months

### Feature 2: Independent Ownership and Management
- Typically owned and operated by entrepreneurs or family members
- Higher degree of autonomy in decision-making
- Owner often directly involved in daily operations
- May be sole proprietorships, partnerships, or privately held companies
- Example: Your local bakery vs a corporate-owned franchise

### Feature 3: Limited Resources but Innovation-Driven
- Smaller workforce, infrastructure, and financial capacity
- However, this often drives innovation and creativity
- Must find creative solutions with limited budgets
- Focus on niche markets or specialized products/services
- Example: Many tech innovations come from startups, not large corporations

**Additional Features (for extra depth):**
- Flat organizational hierarchy with thin management structure
- Local/regional focus rather than global operations
- Personal relationships with customers
- Owner-dependent (business success tied to owner's skills)

## A3: 5 Business Life Cycle Stages - Characteristics (15 marks)

### Stage 1: START-UP / LAUNCH

**Characteristics:**
- Business concept turns into reality
- Sales are low but slowly increasing
- Focus on proving the value proposition
- High initial costs, often operating at a loss
- Simple organizational structure
- Cash flow is critical - may need external funding
- Employees often wear multiple hats
- High risk of failure (90% of startups fail)

**Focus Areas:**
- Product/service development
- Finding first customers
- Building brand identity
- Securing funding (personal savings, family, angel investors, venture capital)

**Example:** Zomato in its early days (2008-2010) starting as a restaurant listing website

---

### Stage 2: GROWTH

**Characteristics:**
- Business gains market acceptance
- Rapid sales growth
- Products/services gaining traction
- Challenge: managing increasing demand while preserving quality
- Start becoming profitable
- Small amounts of free cash flow begin to appear
- Margin expansion begins
- Growth in profits lags behind growth in sales
- Need to hire more staff and build systems

**Focus Areas:**
- Expanding customer base
- Scaling operations
- Potentially securing additional funding
- Strengthening existing client relationships
- Building proper organizational structure

**Example:** Swiggy/Zomato during 2015-2019 expansion across Indian cities

---

### Stage 3: MATURITY

**Characteristics:**
- Business is stable with established market presence
- Solid customer base and consistent revenue
- Major capital spending is behind the business
- Cash generation higher than profit on income statement
- Focus shifts from expansion to maintaining market share
- Competition becomes biggest challenge
- Risk of complacency
- Need to stay relevant through continuous innovation

**Focus Areas:**
- Operational excellence
- Cost optimization
- Revenue diversification
- Maintaining customer satisfaction
- Looking for market gaps to fill strategically

**Example:** Amul - established market leader in dairy, now diversifying into new product categories

---

### Stage 4: RENEWAL / REVIVAL

**Characteristics:**
- Business reinvents itself to avoid decline
- Innovation and adaptation to emerging trends
- May explore new markets or diversify products
- Requires new ideas and change agents
- Investment in new technologies
- This stage is rare - requires significant effort

**Focus Areas:**
- New product development
- More competitive ways of providing customer benefits
- Strategic partnerships or acquisitions
- Digital transformation

**Example:** LEGO faced decline in early 2000s, then restructured and renewed focus on core products - became stronger than before

---

### Stage 5: DECLINE

**Characteristics:**
- Profits, cash flows, and sales start declining
- Loss of competitive advantage
- Product becomes commoditized or redundant
- Losing clients to competitors
- Resources reallocated to maintain profitability
- May need to rethink product lines
- Could consider merging or being acquired

**Focus Areas:**
- Cost-cutting measures
- Exit strategies
- Possible pivot or reinvention
- Selling assets or business units

**Example:** Nokia - once dominant in mobile phones, declined due to slow adoption of smartphones; Kodak - failed to adapt to digital photography

## A4: Objectives for Each Life Cycle Stage (15 marks)

### Start-up Stage Objectives:
1. **Survival** - Simply staying in business and avoiding closure
2. **Customer acquisition** - Getting first customers to validate the product
3. **Cash flow management** - Ensuring enough money to operate
4. **Product-market fit** - Proving the business idea works
5. **Brand awareness** - Making potential customers aware of the business

### Growth Stage Objectives:
1. **Market share expansion** - Growing the customer base rapidly
2. **Revenue growth** - Increasing sales and income
3. **Operational scaling** - Building systems to handle increased demand
4. **Profitability** - Moving from losses to consistent profits
5. **Team building** - Hiring the right people to support growth

### Maturity Stage Objectives:
1. **Profit maximization** - Optimizing operations for maximum returns
2. **Market leadership** - Maintaining dominant position
3. **Customer retention** - Keeping existing customers satisfied
4. **Efficiency improvement** - Reducing costs while maintaining quality
5. **Diversification** - Exploring new revenue streams

### Renewal Stage Objectives:
1. **Innovation** - Developing new products or services
2. **Market adaptation** - Responding to changing customer needs
3. **Digital transformation** - Adopting new technologies
4. **Competitive repositioning** - Finding new ways to stand out
5. **Sustainable growth** - Long-term viability

### Decline Stage Objectives:
1. **Cost reduction** - Cutting expenses to maintain profitability
2. **Asset protection** - Preserving value where possible
3. **Exit planning** - Considering sale, merger, or closure
4. **Cash preservation** - Maintaining liquidity
5. **Stakeholder management** - Managing relationships during difficult times
